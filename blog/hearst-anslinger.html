The Mission · Deep Dive No. 01

One Man. 28 Papers. 90+ Years of Stigma.

Before cannabis was illegal, it was medicine. The story of how William Randolph Hearst and Harry Anslinger manufactured the stigma from a newsroom — and why that weapon is still loaded today.

CategoryHistory · Policy
Read Time~7 min
SourcesCited inline

Cannabis was listed in the U.S. Pharmacopeia from 1850 onward — prescribed for neuralgia, convulsive disorders, pain, and dozens of other conditions. It had been there for 92 years when it was removed. No new science drove that decision. A newspaper baron did.

The DuPont Theory Is a Footnote

Most people who know anything about cannabis prohibition have heard the DuPont angle — synthetic fibers, industrial hemp as a competitive threat to nylon. There's something to it. DuPont's financial interests were not neutral. But that's not where the stigma was manufactured. The stigma was built in a newsroom, and it was built deliberately.

William Randolph Hearst owned one of the largest newspaper chains in American history — 28 papers at peak reach, landing on millions of doorsteps every day. He also owned millions of acres of timber. Hemp paper threatened that investment directly. Hearst had both a motive and an unmatched megaphone. DuPont had lawyers. Hearst had the public.

The Rebranding Campaign

Beginning around 1930, Hearst's papers ran a coordinated wave of stories connecting cannabis to violence, insanity, and moral collapse. The framing was almost always racial — stories featured Black or Mexican characters as perpetrators, connecting the plant to communities that white, middle-class readers were already primed to fear.

"The passage of the Marihuana Tax Act was not based on sound science. It was primarily due to misinformation on the part of William Randolph Hearst and Harry Anslinger's campaign against African-American minorities and Mexican immigrant workers."

— Derek Rosenzweig, PA Legislative Testimony on Cannabis Policy

The word "marijuana" itself was part of the strategy. Cannabis was clinical, familiar, American — it was in the Pharmacopeia, in medicine cabinets, it was not threatening. Marihuana sounded foreign and dangerous. The deliberate rebranding created linguistic distance between Americans and a plant they had been using medicinally for decades. That distance made fear possible.

From Newsprint to Federal Law

The Pipeline: Propaganda to Policy
1850 Cannabis added to the U.S. Pharmacopeia. Prescribed for neuralgia, convulsive disorders, pain, alcoholism, and over a dozen other conditions. Used medically for 87 years.
1930 Harry Anslinger appointed first commissioner of the Federal Bureau of Narcotics. Hearst newspapers begin the coordinated "marihuana" campaign — sensationalized headlines, racial panic, manufactured crisis.
1936 Reefer Madness released — church-funded, Hearst-amplified. The fictional panic reaches theaters nationwide. Public opinion is fully primed.
1937 Anslinger testifies before Congress, citing Hearst newspaper stories as evidence. The Marihuana Tax Act passes. Yellow journalism has become federal policy.
1942 Cannabis removed from the U.S. Pharmacopeia — 92 years after inclusion. Zero new pharmacological evidence supported the removal.
1970 Nixon's Controlled Substances Act places cannabis in Schedule I — alongside heroin, above cocaine and meth. His own commission recommends against it. Nixon overrides them.
Today Schedule I classification remains. Federal research blockade intact. Only 9% of U.S. medical schools include cannabis in curriculum. 84.9% of residents report receiving zero clinical training on it.

The Circular Logic Trap

Here's the part that should make anyone paying attention angry. The Schedule I classification that was built on Hearst's propaganda is now used to block the clinical research that would produce actual evidence. You can't get federal approval to run trials that would demonstrate cannabis has medical value — because the classification says it has no medical value.

⚠

You cannot demand evidence while blocking the research. The Schedule I classification doesn't just reflect ignorance about cannabis — it actively maintains it. That's not an oversight. That's a feature of the system as it was designed.

Anslinger laundered Hearst's yellow journalism into congressional testimony. Congress codified that testimony as law. Nixon then used that law as a political weapon — his own aide later confirmed on record that the War on Drugs was designed to disrupt Black communities and antiwar protesters. The plant was the pretext. The stigma was the weapon.

By the Numbers

87 Years cannabis was in the U.S. Pharmacopeia before criminalization
28 Hearst newspapers amplifying the "marihuana" panic simultaneously
0 New scientific findings that justified removing cannabis from the Pharmacopeia in 1942
9% U.S. medical schools that include cannabis education in their curriculum today

Why This Is Not Ancient History

The classification built on that 1937 foundation is still active law. It still blocks federally funded clinical trials. It's still why your doctor has almost certainly received zero clinical training on cannabinoid medicine. It's still why, when people arrive at a dispensary after the traditional healthcare system fails them, they meet a budtender instead of a clinician — and encounter high-THC products with no guidance instead of balanced, evidence-informed formulations.

The stigma Hearst manufactured didn't just change what people thought about a plant. It built a policy infrastructure that has blocked honest science for nearly a century. And that infrastructure is still running.

Bottom Line

The stigma was manufactured in a newsroom to protect a timber fortune. The science was suppressed by policy built on that stigma. The people paying the price — patients who can't get honest clinical guidance, researchers who can't get federal approval, communities that were targeted from the beginning — are still paying it. That's not a cannabis problem. That's a documented policy failure that started with 28 newspapers and a financial motive.
